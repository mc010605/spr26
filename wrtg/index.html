Healthcare Industry: How a Lack of Directed Cybersecurity Funding Causes 3 Big Problems

By Matthew Chen

Background and a Reflection on the Past 20 Years

Cybersecurity is important for every business. In healthcare, hospitals and providers handle personal health information (PHI), which makes them a target for cyber criminals.

Healthcare institutions often follow cybersecurity rules only when they have to. This can leave systems outdated for years until a major breach forces change.

Who Ultimately Calls the Shots?

In many healthcare organizations, CISOs are the main people responsible for cybersecurity decisions. They help decide what gets funded, what gets prioritized, and how the organization handles security.

The problem is that one person can end up responsible for too many areas at once, which can lead to burnout and weak long-term planning.

Why Healthcare Systems Are Especially Vulnerable

Hospital systems can be large, complicated, and old. When leadership is unstable and budgets are unclear, it becomes harder to keep systems updated and secure.

Problem #1: Financial Losses for Healthcare Institutions

Data breaches in healthcare are expensive. Organizations can lose money from lawsuits, fines, downtime, and recovery costs.

Problem #2: Patient Data Loss

Losing PHI is not just a money problem. Patients can lose privacy, face identity theft, and deal with long-term personal consequences.

Problem #3: Patient Care Quality Reduction

When hospitals lose money and access to systems, patient care can suffer. Ransomware attacks can delay treatment, shut down clinics, and create serious risks for patients.

How Do We Reduce CISO Turnover Rate?

Better funding and a more organized division of responsibilities could help reduce burnout and improve long-term cybersecurity planning.
